When President Droupadi Murmu handed Narayan Vyas his Padma Shri at Rashtrapati Bhavan on **May 25, 2026**, he was 77 years old. Afterward, reporters asked what he was working on. His answer was immediate: "I am still working on these cave paintings." After 37 years at the Archaeological Survey of India, after contributing to UNESCO World Heritage Site nominations for some of India's most important ancient sites, after writing ten books — Dr. Narayan Vyas is still studying the rock shelters of Madhya Pradesh.

Vyas was born on January 5, 1949, in Ujjain, the sacred city on the Shipra River that has been a centre of Indian learning for two thousand years. He trained first in fine arts, then made a deliberate turn toward archaeology — a discipline that would take him underground, into caves, and across the rock-sheltered plateaus of central India for the rest of his working life.

His doctoral research focused on Rani ki Vav, the extraordinary stepped well in Gujarat commissioned in the eleventh century and only recently excavated from sediment. His post-doctoral work (D.Litt.) ranged wider: the rock art sites of Central India, the painted shelters scattered across the Vindhya and Satpura ranges that hold some of the earliest evidence of human presence in the subcontinent.

## Three UNESCO Sites, a Mentor, and 2,000 Artifacts

The defining collaboration of Vyas's early career was with Dr. Vishnu Shridhar Wakankar, the archaeologist who first recorded the Bhimbetka rock shelters in 1957. In 1970, Vyas joined Wakankar's excavation team at Bhimbetka, in Raisen district of Madhya Pradesh, and worked the site for three years. Bhimbetka is now recognised as a place where human beings have lived continuously for more than **300,000 years**; the painted shelters there represent an unbroken artistic tradition stretching from Homo erectus to the early medieval period. In 2003, the site became a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**.

All three sites Vyas gave his deepest professional attention — Bhimbetka, Rani ki Vav, and the Buddhist stupas of **Sanchi** in Madhya Pradesh — are now on the UNESCO World Heritage List. Sanchi earned the designation in 1989, Bhimbetka in 2003, Rani ki Vav in 2014. Few individual archaeologists can claim to have contributed to documentation and preservation work that supported the cases for three separate UNESCO inscriptions across three different states and three different heritage categories.

Vyas retired from ASI in 2009 as Superintending Archaeologist. He did not retire from the discipline. He has since written ten books, including a 2022 monograph on Dr. Wakankar — "Humare Margdarshak Gurudev Dr. Vishnu Shridhar Wakankar" — that recounts excavation by excavation, discovery by discovery, the work done at Dangwada, Ujjain, and Bhimbetka. His home in Bhopal holds a personal collection of more than **2,000 antiques and artefacts**, a physical archive of the India he has spent his life studying.

The Padma Shri is India's fourth-highest civilian honour. It arrives for Narayan Vyas as confirmation of something that the academic world already knew: that the stories written on the walls of Bhimbetka — the herds, the hunts, the dancers — survived because people cared enough to document them, date them, and make the case that they matter. Madhya Pradesh holds Bhimbetka and Sanchi within its borders. The archaeologist who helped put both on the world's map lives in Bhopal and is still working on the paintings.
