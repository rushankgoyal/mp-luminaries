At Madhya Pradesh Bhawan in New Delhi on 17 July 2026, the National Health Authority presented Madhya Pradesh with the award for **Best Performing Large State — Highest Pre-Authorizations with Biometric Authentication** under Ayushman Bharat. The recognition, given at the NHA's Chintan Shivir, went to a state that has quietly built one of the country's most accountable systems for processing health insurance claims — and the officer who runs that system is **Arvind Shah**, Chief Executive Officer of Ayushman Bharat Niramayam Madhya Pradesh.

Punya Salila Srivastava, Secretary in the Union Ministry of Health and Family Welfare, presented the award. NHA Chief Executive Officer Dr. Sunil Kumar Barnwal and Joint Secretary Kiran Gopal Vaska were also present. Deputy Chief Minister Rajendra Shukla, who holds the health portfolio in the state government, said the recognition reflected the hard work of every field worker and hospital administrator involved in the scheme's delivery across MP.

## What Biometric Authentication Actually Does

Ayushman Bharat Pradhan Mantri Jan Arogya Yojana covers hospitalisation costs for families in the bottom 40 percent of the income distribution. Pre-authorisation is the step that happens before treatment begins: a hospital requests approval from the insurer to proceed with a procedure, and the system checks whether the patient is eligible. In MP, that check now requires biometric verification — a fingerprint or iris scan matched against the UIDAI database.

The purpose is specific and practical. Healthcare fraud under government insurance schemes has taken several forms over the years: ghost beneficiaries who do not exist, fake hospitalisation claims, procedures billed but never performed. Biometric authentication at the pre-authorisation stage ties the claim to a real, living, present person. It removes the possibility of processing a claim in someone's name without them physically being there.

MP's achievement is not just in adopting biometrics — it is in the scale at which pre-authorisations with biometric authentication are being processed. The NHA ranked the state first among large states on this metric, which means the technology is not sitting on paper but working at the front lines of district hospitals, empanelled private hospitals, and community health centres across all 55 districts.

For the families the scheme covers, the effect is more reliable access. A system that can be gamed tends to exhaust its resources before it reaches its intended beneficiaries. A system that cannot be gamed can sustain the coverage it promises. In a state of 8.5 crore people — with a large proportion in the scheme's target income bracket — that difference is substantial.

The Chintan Shivir brought together state health officials, insurance administrators, and hospital representatives from across the country to exchange implementation lessons. MP's award at that forum positions the state's approach as a model worth examining — a biometric-first protocol that other large states can study as they look to tighten their own claims pipelines.

For Arvind Shah and the Niramayam team, the recognition lands in the middle of active expansion work. The scheme continues to add beneficiaries, empanel new hospitals, and process rising claim volumes. The national award does not pause any of that; it affirms the direction.
