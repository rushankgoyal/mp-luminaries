On 9 June 2026, delegates from eleven nations arrived in Indore for a five-day summit that will shape how the world's largest agricultural bloc talks about feeding the planet. The man who put Madhya Pradesh at the centre of that conversation: Shivraj Singh Chouhan — son of a Sehore district farmer, four-time Chief Minister of MP, and since June 2024, India's Union Minister of Agriculture and Farmers' Welfare.

Chouhan was born on 5 March 1959 in Jait village, Sehore district, where his family farmed. He went on to earn a gold medal in MA (Philosophy) from Barkatullah University in Bhopal — a detail that says something about a man who has long moved between the practical world of rural livelihoods and the deliberative world of policy. He was jailed during the Emergency as a young RSS worker, fought his way into electoral politics, and eventually became the longest-serving Chief Minister in Madhya Pradesh's history, holding the post through 2005–2018 and again 2020–2023. When the BJP won the 2024 general election, he moved from Bhopal to New Delhi as Agriculture Minister.

His first major act in that national role has been to bring India's BRICS agricultural chairmanship home. The 16th BRICS Agriculture Ministers' Meeting is running in two phases: a three-day technical working group meeting from 9 June, followed by the ministerial sessions on 12 and 13 June — both in Indore.

## The Numbers on the Table

The eleven BRICS nations — Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa, Egypt, Ethiopia, Iran, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Indonesia — hold **roughly 42 per cent** of the world's agricultural land and produce more than **42 per cent** of global agricultural output. Out of approximately **580 million** small and marginal farmers worldwide, about **70 per cent** live in these countries.

Chouhan has set four priority areas for the working group discussions: food security and nutrition; agricultural trade and international cooperation; climate-resilient and sustainable agriculture; and partnerships in innovation and research. A special session on 12 June will focus on "Future Food Security through Women and Youth," an acknowledgement that smallholder farming is changing and that the bloc needs to think ahead of the demographic curve.

The expected outcome of the ministerial days is a joint declaration from all eleven agriculture ministers, outlining shared commitments on food sustainability, farmer welfare, and the use of digital and climate-smart farming technologies. India and Russia held the first bilateral agriculture meeting of the summit on 9 June, setting a precedent for focused country-to-country dialogue running alongside the broader group talks.

For Madhya Pradesh, the hosting is not incidental. The state is India's largest producer of soybean and ranks among the top growers of wheat, chickpea, and lentil. Placing a global food security dialogue in Indore reflects a natural fit between the state's agricultural identity and the summit's agenda — and it is a pointed statement about where MP now sits on India's economic map.

Chouhan has spent thirty years making the case for the Indian farmer — in MP's assembly corridors, in the Lok Sabha, now at an eleven-nation table. Indore this week is the most visible international stage that case has ever had.
