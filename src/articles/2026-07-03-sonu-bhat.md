On June 29, a student from Biaora — the son of a tea seller — boarded a flight to Japan to join one of the world's most competitive school-level science exchanges. He carried four years of research with him.

Sonu Bhat is a Class 10 student at Government Model Higher Secondary School in Biaora, Rajgarh district, Madhya Pradesh. He is participating in the SAKURA Science Programme from June 29 to July 4, 2026, selected by India's Department of Science and Technology to represent the country at Japanese universities and research facilities.

The material that earned him that seat: **Plastomenium**, a composite Sonu developed by combining plastic waste with aluminium. The hybrid aims to be lighter and stronger than conventional materials, with potential applications in aeronautics and spacecraft manufacturing. He began the work in Class 8 and spent four years refining it — working through the INSPIRE-MANAK evaluation ladder from the district level up to a national award.

## From a School Lab in Rajgarh to a National Shortlist

India's INSPIRE-MANAK programme — Innovation in Science Pursuit for Inspired Research, Million Minds Augmenting National Aspirations and Knowledge — is the Department of Science and Technology's flagship award scheme for school-age innovators. Its selection process is rigorous: students compete at district level, advance to state, then face a national panel, with only the strongest projects winning the MANAK award and the opportunity to be sent abroad.

Sonu's Plastomenium project cleared all three stages. He is the first student from Rajgarh district to reach this point and be nominated for the Japan programme.

The research started with a straightforward question: can plastic waste be structurally combined with aluminium to produce a usable composite material? The school's basic laboratory was the only facility available. That constraint shaped the design — Plastomenium was built from the outset as something achievable from accessible waste materials, without specialist equipment. Early tests varied the plastic-to-aluminium ratio and measured tensile strength and heat resistance across batches. What emerged over four years was a composite that held promise at each evaluation.

## A Family's Stake in the Invention

Sonu's father runs a tea stall in Biaora. The family's means are modest, and the project was funded by nothing beyond the school lab and Sonu's persistence. Rajgarh district officials have publicly noted that no student from the district had previously been selected for this programme.

The SAKURA Science Programme is run by Japan's Science and Technology Agency and places Indian students in Japanese laboratories and universities for direct exposure to research environments they would not otherwise reach. The Department of Science and Technology coordinates the INSPIRE-MANAK pipeline specifically to give students at government schools access to this pathway.

Sonu was selected for it from a town without a single research institution — working with what he had, building something good enough to send India's science agency to represent the country in Japan. He is there this week.
