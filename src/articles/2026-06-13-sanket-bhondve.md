On June 4, 2026, the Government of India selected 17 projects for its National Awards for e-Governance. Among them was a platform that MP's urban residents use every day without thinking much about who built it: **e-Nagarpalika with Cyber Security** — the digital backbone of Madhya Pradesh's city governments.

The officer who built it is **Sanket S. Bhondve**, a 2007-batch IAS officer who serves as Commissioner of Urban Administration and Development in Bhopal.

Before e-Nagarpalika 2.0, a resident who needed a trade licence, a water connection, or a property mutation had to navigate each municipal office on its own terms — different counters, different schedules, often different registers. Bhondve's team collapsed that friction into a single platform. It now runs **more than 20 modules**, handles **24 citizen services**, and links directly into national systems like UMANG, so a resident already on government apps does not need yet another login. GIS-based property mapping replaced hand-drawn ledgers, binding property tax records, building approvals, and address data into one consistent layer. AI modules flag anomalies in incoming applications before a human reviews them, cutting the back-and-forth that slows approvals. Data lives in the cloud, making real-time access possible even for smaller municipalities that previously lacked infrastructure to maintain their own servers.

None of this happened by accident. Before taking the Urban Administration charge in mid-2025, Bhondve served on central deputation as Joint Secretary at the Ministry of Electronics and Information Technology — the body that shapes India's national digital infrastructure standards. He returned to MP having seen, from the inside, where state-level digital projects go wrong: weak security, poor interoperability, systems built to check a compliance box rather than solve a citizen's problem.

## A Platform That Got Security Right from the Start

The project's full name — "E Nagar Palika with Cyber Security" — signals a choice that most civic platforms make too late. Building security in from the architecture stage, rather than layering it on afterward, is the difference between a platform citizens can trust and one that becomes a liability after the first breach.

Speaking about the national award selection, Bhondve said it is "a gratifying outcome of the hard work, innovation and commitment of the entire team to establish transparent digital good governance in the state." The formal award ceremony takes place at the 29th National Conference on e-Governance in Jaipur on June 25–26, 2026.

This is MP's second National e-Governance Award this year: the state's AI surveillance system at the Mahakaleshwar Temple complex in Ujjain — a separate project led by a different officer — also won in 2026. Two wins in the same year, in two very different domains of governance, point to something systematic rather than lucky: MP has a group of officers who are thinking about public technology the way it needs to be thought about, and building systems that actually hold up to use.

India has hundreds of e-governance platforms on paper. The National e-Governance Award is selective precisely because it is looking for the ones that hold up in practice — and in 2026, Madhya Pradesh's urban services platform made that list.
