For a long time, getting a prosthetic limb in the Vindhya region meant the same thing for nearly everyone: a journey to Jaipur. Patients from Rewa, Satna, Sidhi, Singrauli and the other districts of this part of Madhya Pradesh had to depend on a specialised institution in Rajasthan for artificial hands, feet and legs — a trek that added weeks of waiting and travel on top of what was already a difficult process.

That changed this week. Deputy Chief Minister Rajendra Shukla inaugurated a modern artificial limb manufacturing unit at the Rewa Red Cross Society, bringing the production and fitting of prosthetics to the region for the first time. The unit is equipped with computer-assisted and sensor-based technology, enabling the manufacture of customised, high-precision prosthetics that can be fitted locally by trained doctors and technicians at the facility.

Shukla, who has represented the Rewa constituency in the Madhya Pradesh Legislative Assembly for multiple terms and has served as Deputy Chief Minister since December 2023, has overseen the state's health portfolio alongside home affairs and minority welfare. He noted that the upgraded facility would produce more precise and technologically advanced artificial limbs, improving mobility, comfort and rehabilitation outcomes for persons with disabilities. Trained medical professionals at the Rewa Red Cross Society can now assess, manufacture and fit prosthetics in one place — cutting the round trip to Rajasthan out of a process that was already physically and financially demanding for many families.

## Rewa as a Medical Hub

Rewa has been positioning itself as a medical services centre for the broader Vindhya region, a landlocked area in south-eastern Madhya Pradesh that is home to several million people. Shukla has spoken publicly about Rewa becoming a centre for specialised healthcare, pointing to recent additions including upgraded blood bank infrastructure, new diagnostic facilities, and now this prosthetics manufacturing unit.

The Vindhya region has historically had thinner access to specialised medical care compared to the state's larger western cities. The Red Cross Society's new unit addresses one of the sharpest of those gaps. A person with a limb disability who previously needed to coordinate transport to Jaipur — with the cost, the time away from home, and the uncertainty about appointment dates — can now access a complete service at a facility in their own district headquarters.

The investment in local manufacturing also has a ripple effect: when a prosthetic needs adjustment or replacement, a return visit is a manageable trip rather than an out-of-state expedition. For disability rehabilitation, continuity of care is often as important as the initial fitting.
