At 12:42 in the morning on 30 May 2026, Jyoti Ratre left her high camp on Pico de Orizaba and started climbing in the dark. The temperature had fallen sharply. The wind was fierce. Far below her lay Mexico's high volcanic plateau; above, the summit ice-field of the highest volcano in North America. After **10 hours and 28 minutes**, she stood on the crater rim at **5,636 metres** — the first mountaineer from Madhya Pradesh to reach this point. She unfurled India's tricolour and dedicated the feat to the rural women of India.

Ratre is 57 years old and runs a clothing boutique in Bhopal. She came to mountaineering late: serious high-altitude climbing began when she was in her late 40s, a decision she built on without state academy support or sponsored expeditions. The funding, planning, and training were her own. Her professional background — running a small business for decades — gave her the organisational discipline that high-altitude climbing demands even when nothing else prepares you for the altitude itself.

## Six Summits, One Goal

In May 2024, at the age of 55, Ratre became the **oldest Indian woman** to summit Mount Everest. That record alone placed her among the country's most accomplished high-altitude climbers. But she did not stop. By June 2026, she has completed **six of the Seven Summits** — the highest peak on each continent: Everest in Asia, Aconcagua in South America, Mount Elbrus in Europe, Kilimanjaro in Africa, Vinson Massif in Antarctica, and Mount Kosciuszko in Australia. The remaining summit is Denali in Alaska, which stands at 6,190 metres and is the highest point in North America.

Pico de Orizaba is a different, separate distinction. At 5,636 metres it is the highest volcano in North America and the third-highest peak on the continent — and scaling it has made Ratre India's senior-most female mountaineer to have hoisted the Tricolour on the highest volcanoes of three continents: Elbrus in Europe, Kilimanjaro in Africa, and now Orizaba in North America. These are not the same as the Seven Summits; they are a parallel category of achievement, and one that few Indian climbers of any age have registered.

The dedication to rural women is a consistent thread running through her recent expeditions. It reflects an awareness that the story — a middle-aged woman from Bhopal quietly becoming one of the country's most decorated high-altitude climbers — carries meaning beyond the record books if it reaches the right people. The women in MP's villages and smaller towns are not the audience for mountaineering statistics. But the image of a 57-year-old from Bhopal standing on an icy volcano at dawn is a different kind of message.

Madhya Pradesh has no deep mountaineering tradition. The state's Himalayan climbers are few; its volcanic-summit climbers, before this month, were none. Ratre's Pico de Orizaba climb closes that gap while she prepares for Denali — the last mountain between her and the full Seven Summits. When she attempts that one, she will be doing it as the oldest Indian woman ever to have reached the top of five other continents' highest peaks. The bar she has set keeps rising.
