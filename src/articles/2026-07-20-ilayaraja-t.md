The Federation of Associations in Indian Tourism and Hospitality holds its annual conclave to bring the country's travel industry into the same room as the officials who set policy for it. At the FAITH Conclave 2026 in New Delhi this week, the official who made the strongest case for a state's tourism potential was **Dr. Ilayaraja T.**, the IAS officer who currently holds four simultaneous tourism roles in Madhya Pradesh: Secretary to the Chief Minister, Secretary of the Tourism Department, Managing Director of the MP State Tourism Development Corporation, and Commissioner of Tourism.

The numbers he carried to New Delhi were not projections. In 2024, Madhya Pradesh recorded **13.41 crore tourist visits** — a **25 percent year-on-year increase** that placed MP among the fastest-growing travel destinations in the country. Within that figure, spiritual tourism alone accounted for 10 crore visitors, with Ujjain receiving 7 crore devotees across its temples and ghats. The state's Regional Tourism Conclaves — held across divisions to draw private investment — generated **₹64,000 crore in investment proposals**, a number that signals commercial confidence in what the tourism infrastructure can support.

## A Career That Moved Through the Difficult Districts

Dr. Ilayaraja T. is 2009-batch IAS, MP cadre. He was posted to his current cluster of tourism roles on 4 May 2026, taking on a portfolio built on the momentum of a sector that had grown significantly under his predecessors. Before tourism, he served as District Collector of Indore — India's cleanest city by the Swachh Survekshan rankings — where he led waste management reforms and renewable energy projects in one of the state's most complex urban environments.

That operational background shows in how MP Tourism is approaching its current priorities. The department is not just counting visitors; it is building the conditions for a different kind of visit. More than **400 homestays** are now operational across the state, generating **₹7 crore in income** for rural host families — a distribution of tourism revenue into communities that previously received little of it.

The eco-tourism work is specific. Kukru Hill Station in Betul district, developed as a "Coffee Valley" destination, gives travellers an alternative to the standard circuit of forts and temples. The "offbeat-plus" strategy — anchored in Mandu, Orchha, and a Kuno–Chandeli wildlife corridor — is designed to pull visitors into parts of the state that have the assets but not yet the footfall.

Looking further ahead, the department is deep in preparation for **Singhsth-2028**, the Kumbh held at Ujjain. Infrastructure investment for that event is estimated at **₹30,000 crore** — a figure that will reshape Ujjain's roads, ghats, accommodation capacity, and transit connections over the next two years.

At FAITH, Dr. Ilayaraja T. held discussions with domestic and international tour operators — the people who decide which destinations appear in their packages. The pitch MP Tourism made to those operators is framed around a single phrase: **Heart of Incredible India**. The geography backs it. The state sits at the centre of the country, within reach of the north and south circuits, and contains the full range of what international visitors come to India for: wildlife, temples, forts, rivers, and festivals. Translating that geography into actual bookings is the work his department is doing now.
