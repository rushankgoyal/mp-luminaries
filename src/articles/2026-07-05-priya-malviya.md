Priya Malviya's father, ASI Ravi Malviya, has spent years in uniform at the Chhindwara Kotwali police station in Madhya Pradesh. His daughter will wear a different one — with wings.

Priya recently cleared the Air Force Common Admission Test (AFCAT), securing **All India Rank 113** and earning selection as a Flying Officer in the Indian Air Force. She is the first woman from Chhindwara district to achieve that rank. She will travel to Hyderabad for a three-year training programme before her formal posting.

## From Engineering to the Cockpit

Priya holds a Bachelor of Engineering in Electronics and Communication from Gujarat Technological University, one of India's larger technical universities. After completing her degree, she focused entirely on AFCAT — the examination that selects officers for the Flying, Technical and Ground Duty branches of the IAF.

The Flying branch is the most demanding path. A national rank of **113** is not a narrow escape from the cut-off; it is a clear, commanding performance against a field of lakhs of candidates. Beyond the written examination, applicants for Flying branch must pass the Computerised Pilot Selection System (CPSS), a rigorous medical board and a Services Selection Board interview — a sequence designed to test aptitude, physical fitness and judgment under pressure. Priya cleared every stage.

Her engineering background in Electronics and Communication maps directly onto the demands of modern aviation. Avionics, communication systems and flight management software are all woven into a pilot's training from day one. The subject was not an accident; it was preparation.

She has said the selection is among the most memorable moments of her life, and that she hopes it encourages other young women to pursue their aspirations with the same certainty.

## A First That Points Toward More

Chhindwara has produced state legislators and district administrators, but a woman Flying Officer in the Indian Air Force from its ranks is a recorded first. The district sits in Madhya Pradesh's southern Satpura plateau, a region not typically associated with military career pipelines for women. That Priya pursued an engineering degree, sat a national-level defence examination and cleared the full selection process from this setting is, on its own, worth recording.

The IAF has been expanding women's roles across all branches. Women officers are now eligible for permanent commission in Flying, a right that did not exist a decade ago. Priya Malviya steps into that expanded space as Chhindwara's first — and, almost certainly, not its last.

Her father's posting to the Chhindwara Kotwali is an ordinary civil duty. But it produced a daughter who watched what service looks like, close up, every morning — and then decided to take it as high as she could go.
