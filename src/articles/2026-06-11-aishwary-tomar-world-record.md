In a sport where champions are separated by tenths of a point, Aishwary Pratap Singh Tomar just separated himself from the entire world. At the Asian Shooting Championships in New Delhi, the 25-year-old from Madhya Pradesh shot a **world-record 362.0** in the men's 50m rifle 3 positions final — defending his continental title and anchoring an all-Indian sweep of the podium.

The 50m rifle 3 positions event is shooting's decathlon: kneeling, prone and standing, each demanding a different body, a different breath, a different mind. To win it is hard. To win it with a score no human has ever recorded, in front of a home crowd, while two compatriots chase you up the leaderboard — that is the stuff careers are remembered for.

## The making of a marksman

Tomar's story begins about as far from an Olympic range as Indian sport gets. Born in 2001 in **Ratanpur village in Khargone district**, he comes from a farming family — a boy who grew up around fields and cattle, not imported ammunition and electronic targets. Madhya Pradesh's state shooting academy in Bhopal spotted and shaped his talent, and the state has been collecting dividends ever since.

The New Delhi gold is his **fifth individual medal at the Asian Championships** — bronze in Doha in 2019, gold in Changwon in 2023, silver in Jakarta in 2024, gold again in Shymkent in 2025, and now a world-record gold at home. Few Indian shooters of any generation have stacked continental podiums with that consistency.

Consistency, in fact, is the word coaches keep reaching for with Tomar. Rifle shooting punishes drama and rewards routine; its champions are built from thousands of identical mornings. That a farmer's son from Khargone has out-routined the best marksmen on the planet's most competitive shooting continent says everything about the discipline beneath the medal.

For Madhya Pradesh, Tomar is proof of a pipeline — evidence that a state academy in Bhopal can take village talent to the top of the world rankings. For the rest of us, he is a reminder that world records do not require big-city childhoods. Sometimes they begin in Ratanpur.
