At the 20th Annual Conference of Vision 2020 India, held in Bengaluru from 10 to 12 July 2026, the organisers called the name of a hospital most attendees had never visited: Sewa Sadan Eye Hospital, a facility tucked inside the Bairagarh colony of Bhopal that has been providing care to patients who could afford little else for nearly four decades. Hospital Administrator Anusha Tiwari stepped forward to accept the **National Innovative Eye Care Practices Award** alongside Director Kushal Dharmani, dedicating the honour to every doctor, nurse, optometrist, and support worker who had made it possible.

The conference, organised by Sankara Eye Hospitals and bringing together leading ophthalmologists, researchers, and administrators from across India, awarded the Innovative Eye Care Practices recognition to institutions that demonstrate measurably better approaches to serving patients. That a Bhopal hospital built on service to the poor came home with a national prize from this gathering is an outcome worth tracing back to its roots.

Sewa Sadan Eye Hospital opened in 1987, three years after the Bhopal gas tragedy — the worst industrial disaster in history — had struck the city. The toxic methyl isocyanate gas that leaked from the Union Carbide plant on the night of 2–3 December 1984 caused severe eye damage across affected communities: inflammation, impaired vision, and lasting blindness were among the most widespread consequences for survivors. The hospital was established, with the blessings of His Holiness Sant Hirdaram Sahibji, specifically to provide eye care to those still living with those effects and to the broader poor population of Madhya Pradesh.

## Thirty-Eight Years of Affordable Care

The hospital has since grown into a multi-specialty facility offering treatment across **eight sub-specialties** of ophthalmology, while keeping its founding mission intact. Throughout the year, teams travel to remote and rural areas of Madhya Pradesh to run free eye camps, where they perform cataract removal surgeries — including intraocular lens implants — for patients who cannot reach a city or pay for standard hospital fees. At the Bairagarh facility, patients receive treatment at nominal charges, regardless of caste, income, or background.

This kind of work rarely surfaces at national conferences. It accumulates invisibly in village health records and in the sight restored to farmers, labourers, and older adults who had expected to go blind simply because they had no other option. The Vision 2020 India award, given at a conference attended by India's top eye care professionals, brought that accumulation into public view.

For Anusha Tiwari, managing a hospital whose daily operations are defined by the tension between quality care and genuinely low fees, the recognition in Bengaluru signals something practical: that the model works. Bhopal carries the historical weight of 1984 in ways that are still visible in health outcomes. Sewa Sadan Eye Hospital has spent nearly four decades offering one answer to that weight, and a national award is now on record to say it was the right kind of answer.
