When Kailash Chandra Pant walked into Rashtrapati Bhavan on **May 25, 2026**, to receive his Padma Shri from President Droupadi Murmu, he was 90 years old. He had been promoting the Hindi language for more than six decades. Back in Bhopal, his magazine *Akshara* was still being published — as it has been every two months since 2003.

Pant was born on April 26, 1936, in Mhow, the Madhya Pradesh garrison town also known as the birthplace of Dr. B.R. Ambedkar. He trained for a government career, but at some point he made a different calculation: the Hindi language, the journalism built around it, and the readers who depended on it needed champions more than they needed one more civil servant.

He left his government post and did not look back.

His first venture was *Shiksha Pradeep*, a monthly journal focused on education through the Hindi medium. He followed it with *Jandharma*, a weekly newspaper. In 2000, from Indore, he launched *Duragami Outlook* — a literary magazine committed to the long view of Hindi's place in Indian intellectual life. In 2003 he moved operations to Bhopal and started *Akshara*, which has run without interruption for over twenty years.

Alongside the magazines, Pant has served as General Secretary of the **Madhya Pradesh Rashtrabhasha Prachar Samiti**, the organisation that coordinates advocacy for Hindi as a national language across the state — organising events, supporting Hindi writers, and pressing institutions to take the language seriously in domains where English tends to crowd it out.

## A Career That Earned the Nehru Literacy Award — and Now a Padma

Earlier in his career, Pant received the Nehru Literacy Award from President APJ Abdul Kalam, in recognition of his work in education access through the Hindi medium. The Padma Shri, awarded for 2026 in the category of Literature and Education, is the larger national acknowledgment of a career that kept showing up for the same cause across seven decades.

> "Not only a matter of pride for me, but for others involved in education and literature."
>
> — Kailash Chandra Pant, on receiving the Padma Shri

Pant represents a kind of figure that national conversations rarely surface: the writer-editor who is not glamorous, not controversial, and not seeking a platform beyond the page. Four magazines over sixty years. A weekly, a monthly, two bi-monthlies. Year after year, putting Hindi journalism into readers' hands in a state where it genuinely matters.

The Padma Shri arrived later in his life than it might have. But it arrived, and it arrived for exactly the right work: the patient, unglamorous effort to keep a language alive, precise, and in print. For MP's literary community, the recognition confirms what those inside the field have known for years — that Kailash Chandra Pant has been one of its most consistent builders.
