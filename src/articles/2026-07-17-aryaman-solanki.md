On 17 July 2026, the National Testing Agency published the NEET-UG 2026 results, and one name stood out from Madhya Pradesh: Aryaman Singh Solanki of Jabalpur, who scored **696 out of 720** marks and secured **All India Rank 46** — making him the state's top performer among nearly 20 lakh candidates who appeared in India's most competitive undergraduate medical entrance examination.

The results came from the June 21 re-examination, conducted after the original NEET-UG sitting was cancelled following concerns about the integrity of the examination process. For Aryaman, the re-test turned into an opportunity. "Re-NEET proved beneficial for me," he told reporters, adding that it gave him a second chance to prepare more carefully and perform closer to his potential. Nearly **11.21 lakh candidates** qualified for admission to MBBS, BDS, AYUSH, and allied courses this year.

## A Consistent Approach, Not a Marathon

Aryaman's preparation did not follow the common model of exhaustive study hours. According to his family, he typically studied for just **four to five hours a day** — but maintained that schedule with deliberate regularity and focused on building conceptual clarity rather than rote memorisation. He began serious preparation from Class 11, working steadily without sudden bursts of effort or pre-exam cramming.

Both his parents are doctors: his father, Dr Fanishendra Solanki, is a urologist, and his mother, Dr Anupama Solanki, is also a medical professional. Far from feeling pressured to follow in their footsteps, Aryaman described a household that encouraged open thinking. "Papa never said that I have to become a urologist," he noted. "He always tells me to keep exploring new choices." His father echoed the family's approach: "We used to talk about this every day — that whatever happens, happens for the best."

Aryaman's target now is an MBBS seat at the **All India Institute of Medical Sciences (AIIMS), Delhi** — the institution that draws India's highest-scoring medical aspirants. His AIR of 46 positions him comfortably within AIIMS Delhi's admission range.

Jabalpur, a city known for its defence establishments, High Court, and river geography but less often in the national spotlight for academic rankings, sent one of the country's top-46 medical aspirants this year. The result adds to a longer thread of talent emerging from central Madhya Pradesh, where students increasingly compete at the highest national level without necessarily studying in metropolitan coaching centres.

For a state that already has AIIMS Bhopal within its borders, the presence of Madhya Pradesh students among India's medical toppers matters to both healthcare planning and the wider aspiration that quality medicine can be built from the inside — not only imported.
