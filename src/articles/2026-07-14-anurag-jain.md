Inside the convention venue in Bhopal on 13 July 2026, Chief Secretary Anurag Jain opened the MP Tech Growth Conclave 3.0 with a number already in the bank: **₹12,000 crore** in digital and technology sector investments had been implemented in the state since the previous edition of the conclave. By the time the day's announcements were tallied, the room had added proposals worth more than **₹40,000 crore** — covering global capability centres, data centres, semiconductor manufacturing, food processing, and pharmaceuticals, with **34,000 jobs** expected to follow.

The day's investment board read like a cross-section of the new industrial geography of inland India. A Spain-based group reaffirmed plans to establish a data centre in Madhya Pradesh. A semiconductor company announced a **₹6,000-crore** investment. Canada's McCain Foods committed **₹3,800 crore** to food processing infrastructure. UK's Hellion announced a **₹3,000-crore** pharmaceutical project. Chief Minister Dr. Mohan Yadav described the state as an emerging key destination for technology investment; Jain's role as the senior civil servant in the room was to make that claim specific — to translate political ambition into implementation timelines and cleared-ground commitments.

Jain, a 1989-batch IAS officer who took charge as Madhya Pradesh's 35th Chief Secretary in October 2024, has made industrial investment a structural rather than episodic priority. The Tech Growth Conclave 3.0 was not a standalone event but the visible apex of sustained groundwork across regulatory approvals, land availability, and infrastructure readiness.

## India's First Round-the-Clock Renewable Energy Tender

Three weeks before the conclave, on 26 June 2026, the Rewa Ultra Mega Solar Limited (RUMSL) — the state agency whose Rewa solar project once set a world cost benchmark — published a tender that clean-energy specialists have called a first for India. The premise was straightforward but technically ambitious: instead of specifying a technology — solar panels, wind turbines, battery banks — RUMSL gave developers full freedom to bid using any combination of renewable sources and storage solutions, whether battery energy storage systems, pumped storage projects, or other emerging options.

The single non-negotiable: reliable power delivery across the full 24-hour cycle, with **90% guaranteed annual availability**, including during peak demand hours from 6 pm to 9 am.

The project was designed under Jain's oversight. The International Finance Corporation (IFC) is advising the transaction; Trilegal, PwC India, Deloitte, and SgurrEnergy are among the technical and legal advisors. The initiative was announced by Chief Minister Yadav at the World Economic Forum in Davos before being formally tendered in June; a pre-bid meeting followed in New Delhi in early July.

The connection between the two initiatives is not incidental. Madhya Pradesh's pitch to global technology companies — data-centre operators, chipmakers, pharmaceutical manufacturers — depends on reliable, affordable power. The RUMSL tender is designed to build that supply before the demand arrives. Anurag Jain's administration is constructing both ends of the pipeline simultaneously: securing the investors at the conclave in Bhopal while locking in the energy infrastructure in the field.
