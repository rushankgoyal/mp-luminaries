In Ujjain last February, the ancient city that once served as the capital of King Vikramaditya's realm transformed itself into a **39-day living heritage festival**. Streets filled with folk performers, seminars examined the mathematics and astronomy of classical India, and the formal inaugural of Vikramotsav 2026 on 15 February drew audiences from across the country. By the time the festival concluded on 24 March, its programmes and outreach activities had reached more than **177 million people** — numbers that place it among the largest cultural events in India's recent memory.

That scale was not accidental. Behind Vikramotsav stands the Maharaja Vikramaditya Research Institute (MKVRI), which Shriram Tiwari founded and still directs. Tiwari also serves as Culture Advisor to Madhya Pradesh's Chief Minister. He has spent years building the institute around one idea: that Vikramaditya's India — a civilisation that standardised the Vikram Samvat calendar, produced pioneering work in astronomy, and sustained the nine scholars known as the Navaratnas — deserves to be brought before contemporary audiences with the same seriousness as any globally recognised heritage programme.

## Three Prizes in a Single Year

The event industry has taken notice. On **15 and 16 July 2026**, at the Hindustan 2.0 National Chintan Shivir and Expo in Dehradun, the Business International Trade Association (BITA) presented Vikramotsav 2026 with its Gold Award in the "Government Event of the Year" category. It was the **third national recognition the festival had collected in 2026 alone**.

The earlier two came through the WOW Live Awards, India's peer-reviewed event industry prize. Vikramotsav 2026 won the Gold Award in "Cultural Live Event of the Year" and the Silver Award for "Best Government Integration for a Live Event" — the second prize recognising how government departments were woven into a coherent live experience rather than added as a bureaucratic afterthought.

During the festival itself, Madhya Pradesh announced the **Emperor Vikramaditya International Award**, worth **₹1.01 crore**, to be given annually to individuals anywhere in the world who contribute to preserving and advancing India's classical heritage. The prize itself is a product of Vikramotsav's ambition: rather than settling for a well-attended local celebration, MKVRI used the festival to create a lasting institutional mechanism for recognising cultural scholarship globally.

For Madhya Pradesh, the compounding awards matter because Ujjain is both a pilgrimage site and an administrative city with genuine tourism infrastructure. A festival that wins industry recognition as the country's best government cultural event attracts programming partners, visiting scholars, and media coverage that a government press release alone cannot generate. Tiwari's model — grounding a large-scale public event in specific historical scholarship while delivering it as mass entertainment — is one that other state cultural departments will study. Three national awards in a calendar year is the evidence that the model works.
