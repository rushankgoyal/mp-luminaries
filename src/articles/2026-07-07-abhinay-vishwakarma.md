On 29 June 2026, certifying auditors signed off on a set of documents that marked something genuinely new for Madhya Pradesh policing. Sixteen units of the Katni district police — **thirteen police stations** and three offices — received ISO 9001:2015 certification, the international standard for quality management systems. The certified offices include the Superintendent of Police's own office and the SDOP offices of Vijayraghavgarh and Sleemnabad. Among the thirteen police stations certified are Kotwali, Kuthla, Madhavnagar, Traffic, and NKJ thanas.

The officer who drove it there is **Abhinay Vishwakarma**, a 2019-batch IPS officer serving as Katni's SP. He joined the district with a clear intent: to make police accountability something that could be measured and verified by an external authority, not just self-reported internally.

ISO 9001:2015 is not an award that a police force applies for and receives. It is a certification that an organisation earns by demonstrating, to independent auditors, that its processes meet a defined standard across six core areas: quality management, citizen satisfaction, scientific record-keeping, effective use of resources, time-bound service delivery, and continuous improvement. Auditors inspect procedures, review records, and interview staff. They certify the system, not the intention.

## What the Certification Required

For a police station, that standard translates into concrete changes. Each thana needed to document exactly how a citizen complaint is received, how it is logged, within what timeframe an initial response is given, and how the complainant is kept informed as the case progresses. Before the drive, a person walking into one Katni thana might find a different procedure than at the next. After it, each station operates to a documented, auditable standard.

The SP office being included in the certified list is significant. Vishwakarma applied the quality management framework to his own office — meaning the accountability structure is not one that the district's top officer handed down from outside. It starts at the top.

Preparing sixteen units for simultaneous certification required months of internal work. Officers at each station were trained on quality management concepts, record-keeping protocols, and citizen service standards. Physical infrastructure — lighting, waiting areas, complaint boxes, notice boards — was brought up to standard. Complaint tracking was digitised. The goal was that a citizen visiting any Katni thana would encounter the same baseline of organised, responsive service.

The ISO 9001:2015 certification is not a terminal achievement. It requires a re-audit cycle and obliges the certified organisation to maintain and improve its systems continuously. That built-in accountability is part of what makes the standard meaningful for a police force: it cannot simply pass once and revert.

For Madhya Pradesh, the scale of Katni's achievement — sixteen units certified in a single district at once — sets a visible reference point. For citizens in Katni, the change is more direct: the police station down the road now operates under a set of commitments that an international standard-setting body has formally verified.
