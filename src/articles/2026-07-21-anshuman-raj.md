On 13 July 2026, more than five hundred cyber specialists from fifty countries filed into the National Forensic Sciences University campus in Gandhinagar for the 11th INTERPOL Digital Forensics Expert Group meeting. Inside those three days sat something new: the first-ever International Investigators' Summit and Competition, a pressure-test that asked **122 practitioners from more than 18 countries** to work realistic, scenario-based cases against the clock. When the rankings came out on 15 July, one team stood second: Madhya Pradesh's own Computer Emergency Response Team, MP-CERT.

At the front of that team was **Anshuman Raj**, Director of MP-CERT, flanked by Vaibhav Srivastav — a Superintendent of Police with the Anti-Terrorist Squad who doubles as Joint Director — and five specialists: Ambar Pande (Operations Head), Abhishek Shroti, Vishal Shukla, Yogesh Pandit, and Rishabh Choubey. They faced practitioners from national cyber agencies, police forensic labs, and intelligence units across the globe, and came away with the first runner-up position.

## What MP-CERT Does — and Why This Placement Matters

MP-CERT operates under the Madhya Pradesh Department of Science & Technology. Its daily work is defending the state's digital infrastructure: monitoring threats, coordinating responses to cyber incidents affecting government departments, and building forensic capacity across MP's police force. The team's appearance in Gandhinagar placed it alongside some of the world's most specialised institutions in AI-enabled digital investigation.

The competition was not theoretical. Participants worked scenario-based cases designed to mirror real investigations — recovering evidence from compromised devices, reconstructing digital crime scenes, and applying AI tools to forensic analysis. Finishing second in such a field, where many entrants came from full-time national cyber agencies with far larger resources, is a precise measure of where Madhya Pradesh's capability now stands.

The summit itself was inaugurated by Union Home Minister Amit Shah and drew delegations from more than fifty countries. For INTERPOL's Digital Forensics Expert Group, this was the eleventh gathering; the investigators' competition, however, was a first — making this the inaugural occasion on which any state-level cyber unit from India claimed a top-two finish at such a contest.

That detail carries weight beyond a single trophy. Madhya Pradesh is not a coastal technology hub. It does not host the density of IT campuses found in Bengaluru or Hyderabad. What this result shows is that a focused, well-directed state team can build competitive expertise in a field that will only grow more consequential as digital crime scales up. For every district administration that stores citizen data, for every court that now handles electronic evidence, having a Bhopal-based unit trained to INTERPOL standard is a resource that will be drawn on long after the ceremony photographs fade.
