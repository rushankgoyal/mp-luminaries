On 3 July 2026, a workshop in Bhopal brought together UN Women's India Country Representative, Shoko Ishikawa, and the officials running Madhya Pradesh's Safe Tourism Destinations for Women programme. Ishikawa called it "a model for inclusive tourism" in India — the clearest international endorsement yet of an initiative that has been quietly reengineering how Madhya Pradesh's historic sites handle the safety of women who visit them.

The programme is the work of Sheo Shekhar Shukla, a 1994-batch IAS officer of the Madhya Pradesh cadre who serves as Additional Chief Secretary for Tourism and Managing Director of the MP Tourism Board. The Safe Tourism Destinations for Women (STDW) framework now covers **50 destinations** across **20 clusters and 33 districts** — from Khajuraho and Orchha to smaller heritage and pilgrimage towns that draw significant visitor numbers but have not historically had coordinated women's safety infrastructure.

## Safety as Infrastructure

STDW is not a helpline and a pamphlet. It is built as operational infrastructure. The programme has trained approximately **30,000 women** as community social capital — tourism-adjacent workers, guides, local representatives — who form a distributed support network across covered destinations. A separate stream has enrolled **40,000 girls in self-defence training**, and **6,500 rural women** have been placed into formal hospitality industry roles, creating economic stakes in visitor safety alongside the structural ones.

The physical layer of the programme runs on **₹19 crore** worth of AI-enabled camera surveillance deployed across the major tourist sites. The cameras feed a monitoring system that the Tourism Board operates in coordination with district administration and police.

UN Women published a formal Women's Safety Audit of the initiative in February 2026, providing a methodology framework that has since circulated among other Indian states looking for replicable models. The July 3 Bhopal workshop was a follow-up step: translating the audit's findings into implementation guidance and state-level policy commitments. MP Tourism Board has set an annual target of declaring one destination **fully managed by women** — from ticketing and guiding to hospitality operations — as a proof-of-concept for what the deepest version of the programme can look like.

The framing matters. Most tourism safety initiatives are reactive: respond when something happens, communicate that a destination is safe. Shukla's approach has been to treat safety as a form of infrastructure that, like roads or electricity, requires investment, maintenance, and visible accountability. The UN Women audit treated it seriously enough to study and publish. Ishikawa's endorsement at the Bhopal workshop took it further: a signal that what Madhya Pradesh built at the sub-district level is now being looked at from Geneva.

For a state whose tourism marketing has long led with tigers, temples, and tribal heritage, building an international reputation for women's safety infrastructure represents a different kind of ambition — one that will matter more to the next generation of independent travellers choosing where to go.
