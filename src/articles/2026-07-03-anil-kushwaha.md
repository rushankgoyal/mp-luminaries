On June 28, Prime Minister Narendra Modi devoted several minutes of his 135th Mann Ki Baat episode to a group from Biaora, Rajgarh. They had built a bridge — not from steel or concrete, but from stuffed plastic bottles.

Anil Kushwaha heads the Paryavaran Premi Sanrakshan Samiti (Environment Lovers Conservation Committee) in Biaora. About six months ago, the committee set out to answer a practical question that most small Indian towns face but rarely act on: what to do with the polythene bags and plastic packaging that pile up on streets and outside homes. Their answer was to stop treating it as waste and start treating it as raw material.

The process they devised requires no machinery and no budget. An empty plastic bottle becomes a mould. Polythene bags, wrappers and scrap plastic are stuffed tightly inside and compressed until the bottle is as hard as stone. That compressed bottle is an eco-brick — durable, stackable and built from material that would otherwise block a drain or burn in a rubbish pile.

## Twenty Tonnes, Seven Hundred Bricks, One Bridge

The committee's output over six months is specific and measurable: **more than 20 tonnes** of plastic and recyclable waste collected across Biaora; **over 700 eco-bricks** produced. Three hundred families now participate regularly, setting aside their household polythene and handing it over to the committee rather than discarding it.

The eco-bricks have gone into park benches, garden paths and decorative structures in public spaces around Biaora. Most visibly, the committee constructed a small bridge — a functional crossing built entirely from the town's collected plastic. That bridge is what Prime Minister Modi singled out in Mann Ki Baat episode 135: a demonstration, he said, of circular economy principles put into practice at neighbourhood scale, without external support or specialist technology.

Kushwaha has spoken about how the first months were hard. Persuading families to change long-standing habits — to separate plastic waste rather than mix it with everything else — required the committee to go door to door, show residents what an eco-brick looked like and explain that what they were throwing away had value. That repeated, unglamorous effort is what produced the three hundred participating households and the **20-plus tonnes** of collected material.

## A Model Rajgarh Did Not Expect to Export

Rajgarh district is not a name that usually enters national conversations on environmental innovation. After June 28 it is.

The Mann Ki Baat feature has brought visitors from other towns to Biaora to understand how the eco-brick programme works, with groups asking whether it can be replicated in their own localities. The method is simple enough that the answer is almost certainly yes: the plastic is already there, the bottles are already there, and the labour is people who live nearby and are willing to show up.

What Anil Kushwaha organised — over six months, in a small town with no resources beyond its own refuse — is a working model for how a community can close its own plastic waste loop. The bridge is real. **Twenty tonnes** is real. PM Modi mentioned them by name. For Biaora, and for Madhya Pradesh's many towns with the same polythene problem, that matters.
