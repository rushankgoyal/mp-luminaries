On June 1, 2026, Pradhuman Singh Tomar moved out of his house. Not far — only across the road to a public park in Gwalior, where he pitched a waterproof tent and installed a fan. He commutes on a solar-charged e-bike. He keeps his shirts un-ironed. For the entire month, the Madhya Pradesh Energy Minister has pledged to live without air conditioning, without a petrol vehicle, and without the ordinary comforts of a senior minister's life. The reason he gives is direct: an Energy Minister who asks citizens to save power should show he means it.

The gesture would matter less if the policy record behind it were thin. It is not.

Madhya Pradesh has declared 2026 as **Kisan Kalyan Varsh** — the Year of Farmer Welfare — and Tomar's energy department has made one of the most concrete deliveries of that year. Under the initiative, MP Central Discom connected **more than 1.1 lakh** new agricultural pump connections across the state, each for a fee of just **₹5**. In a state where smallholder farming dominates large tracts of the countryside, a cheap, reliable pump connection means the difference between one harvest season and two. Farmers who had been waiting for connections — sometimes for years — received them under the scheme without paying large upfront installation charges.

## India's Largest Farm-Solar Network

The larger structural shift came on March 31, 2026, when Madhya Pradesh signed power purchase agreements for **4,022 MW** of decentralised solar capacity under the Krishi Mitra Surya Feeder Yojana — one of the biggest feeder solarisation programmes in India to date. The projects will be built across **493 substations**, with individual plant sizes ranging from 1.6 MW to 18.15 MW, putting solar generation directly onto the feeders that supply agricultural consumers.

The tariffs discovered under competitive bidding: between **₹2.40 and ₹2.85 per unit**. Those numbers are among the lowest ever achieved for distributed renewable energy in India — and they were reached without any subsidy support from the state or central government. Daytime solar power will go directly to agricultural feeders during the hours farmers need to run their pumps, cutting their costs and simultaneously easing load on the state's distribution network.

MP Transco, the state transmission company, separately reported that it held its transmission losses to **2.60%** for FY2025-26 — comfortably below the 2.75% benchmark set by the Madhya Pradesh Electricity Regulatory Commission. Maintaining that figure across a grid that is rapidly absorbing solar generation, connecting thousands of new pump consumers, and supplying growing industrial and urban load is a genuine operational achievement. Tomar credited the result to coordinated teamwork among engineers and ground staff.

Tomar has represented the Gwalior constituency in the Madhya Pradesh Vidhan Sabha and has served as Energy Minister since December 25, 2023. His ministry is not usually a high-visibility portfolio during election cycles. But farm power and transmission efficiency are the foundations on which MP's agricultural economy runs. The 1.1 lakh new connections, the 4,022 MW solar deal, the sub-benchmark transmission losses — and the minister sleeping under a fan in a park to make the point — add up to a record that the state's farmers will be living with long after the tent comes down.
