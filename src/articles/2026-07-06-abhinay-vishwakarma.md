On 29 June 2026, the Superintendent of Police office in Katni received a certificate that most government offices in India never bother to pursue: an ISO 9001:2015 quality management certification. So did thirteen police stations and two Sub-Divisional Police Officer offices scattered across the district. All sixteen units, in a single ceremony.

The push came from Abhinay Vishwakarma, a 2019-batch IPS officer who has served as Katni's Superintendent of Police, and whose method has been deliberate: inspect the stations himself, walk through the complaint rooms and record rooms with his staff, and hold the entire district force to the same standard he would apply to a well-run institution.

The certification is not a trophy. ISO 9001:2015 is an international standard for Quality Management Systems — it requires an organisation to map how it delivers services, track whether citizens are satisfied, document its processes, and put in place a system for continuous improvement. For a police station, this means time-bound complaint registration, clean and accessible premises, scientifically maintained records, and a feedback loop that lets the public say whether their problem was actually addressed.

## What 16 Units Means on the Ground

The sixteen newly certified bodies are: the SP Office, the SDOP offices of Vijayraghavgarh and Sleemnabad, and all thirteen police stations in the district — Kotwali, Kuthla, Madhavnagar, Traffic, NKJ, Sleemnabad, Umaria Pan, Bahoriband, Dhimarkheda, Reethi, Badwara, Barhi, and Vijayraghavgarh.

Certifying two stations at a time, as some districts have done, delivers a headline. Certifying a whole district's worth of stations at once means the standard is structural, not cosmetic. The certification ceremony in Katni was attended by Additional Director General of Police (Jabalpur Zone) Pramod Verma, alongside the District Collector and other senior officers — a signal of how seriously the state administration received the achievement.

Vishwakarma's approach involved personally inspecting stations including Kuthla and Madhavnagar well before the certification audit, working alongside officers and constables on cleanliness and documentation rather than issuing orders from a distance. The certification process touched every measurable dimension of public service: workplace safety, resource utilisation, accountability mechanisms, and grievance redress.

> "A framework that reminds us every day that public trust has to be earned repeatedly, through action and service," Vishwakarma said of the ISO standard.

## Why It Matters for MP

Katni is not a glamorous posting. It sits in the coal-belt corridor of eastern Madhya Pradesh, a district where law and order challenges are real and bureaucratic reform rarely makes national news. That is precisely why this achievement stands out.

District police forces across India are often measured by crime statistics and crime clearance rates. What Vishwakarma has built in Katni adds a different measure: whether the institution meets a global benchmark for service quality, and commits to improving it year on year. If the model holds, Katni could become the template that other MP district forces use when they begin the same journey — a quiet reform that travels without fanfare, one station at a time.
