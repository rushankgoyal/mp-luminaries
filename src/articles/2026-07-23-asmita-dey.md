The woman competing for India in the women's -48 kg judo event at Glasgow's SEC Centre today started out running 800 metres on a school track in Belonia, a small town in South Tripura. That shift — from sprints to throws — turned Asmita Dey into the most consistent Indian judoka in her weight category and the **first athlete from Tripura to win an international judo medal**.

Dey was born on **22 March 2003**, the daughter of a man who runs a bicycle repair shop in Belonia. She enrolled at the Tripura Sports School in 2015, initially pursuing athletics. A coach saw something in her beyond running times and introduced her to judo. She trained under Bina Debnath and later Manik Lal Deb, quickly outgrowing the junior circuit. The government's Target Olympic Podium Scheme identified her as a development athlete, and she was placed at the SAI National Centre of Excellence in Bhopal, where coach Yashpal Solanki has worked with her ever since.

## A Medal Timeline Built Year by Year

The breakthrough came in July 2022 at the Asian Cadet and Junior Judo Championships in Bangkok: a bronze medal in the -48 kg category — the first time any judoka from Tripura had reached the podium at an international meet. She built from there.

In July 2023 she won **gold** at the Junior Asia Cup Judo Championship in Macau, and added a silver at the Asian Open in Kuwait City later that year. In 2024 she earned bronze at the Commonwealth Judo Championships. Then in 2025 she won **gold** at the African Open in Casablanca, Morocco, demonstrating she can compete and win across continents and age groups.

She entered 2026 in the same form, appearing at the Asian Senior Championships as final preparation before Glasgow. Four judokas from SAI NCOE Bhopal were selected for the 2026 Commonwealth Games, and Dey is among them — competing in the women's -48 kg event that gets underway at the SEC Centre today.

What Dey's presence at Glasgow says about Madhya Pradesh's contribution to Indian sport is worth noting. The SAI NCOE Bhopal has become a working pipeline for judo: four of the judokas India has fielded at Glasgow trained under the same roof in Bhopal. For a state that is not traditionally associated with the sport, that is a meaningful contribution — built not through headlines but through years of daily mat time and coaching continuity. The girl from Belonia is today one of its best examples.
