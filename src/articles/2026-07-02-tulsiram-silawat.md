When monsoon clouds gather over Madhya Pradesh this July, they will fall on a landscape that looks measurably different from last year. Over one hundred days from 19 March to 30 June 2026, the state's Water Resources Department — led by Minister Tulsiram Silawat — completed **3.62 lakh water conservation works** across all 55 districts: ponds desilted, stepwells restored, check dams repaired, rivers cleared of encroachment, and Amrit Sarovars created where cracked earth had stood before. The total investment: **Rs 10,514 crore**.

The scale is hard to picture. Three lakh sixty-two thousand individual works, each a pond, a well, or a dam, each designed to hold and recharge water for the villages around it. Taken together, they add water storage capacity that will serve farmers, livestock, and households through droughts and lean months. By the department's own accounting, this is the largest single-season water conservation push in Madhya Pradesh's recorded history.

## What the Minister Actually Did

Silawat is a six-term MLA from Sanwer constituency in Indore district — a Scheduled Caste seat he has retained through successive elections with consistent margins. He has served as Water Resources Minister since 2018, across both the Shivraj Singh Chouhan and Mohan Yadav governments. That continuity is unusual and reflects a genuine command of the technical vocabulary of irrigation and groundwater management, not just political accommodation.

For the Jal Ganga Samvardhan Abhiyan's third phase in 2026, Silawat personally rolled out the campaign in Indore district from village Jhalria and addressed the state-level launch ceremony at the ISKCON Temple in Indore. His department then coordinated across five separate agencies — rural development, urban local bodies, the forest department, irrigation and industrial bodies — to pursue a single campaign target across 55 districts simultaneously. That kind of cross-departmental coordination, unglamorous and prone to collapse under bureaucratic friction, is where water conservation campaigns usually stall. This one did not.

The campaign also worked as a public movement. Schools, colleges, corporates, religious institutions, and gram panchayats were drawn in alongside government machinery. Construction Mirror and the Water Digest both noted the breadth of participation, describing it as a "people's movement for water preservation" — and in this case, the numbers justify the phrase.

Madhya Pradesh enters the monsoon season with replenished water bodies and restored groundwater recharge sites. That translates to more water available next summer, reduced crop losses when the rain is uneven, and less dependence on emergency water tankers in the districts that historically run dry by March. For a landlocked, agriculture-dependent state where groundwater depletion is the single largest constraint on rural income, these are not soft, symbolic gains.

The works completed under Silawat's campaign will compound quietly through the groundwater table over the next several years — the kind of outcome that does not make for dramatic press releases but shows up eventually in well-depth surveys, paddy yields, and the absence of drinking-water crises in village habitations that used to make headlines every April.
