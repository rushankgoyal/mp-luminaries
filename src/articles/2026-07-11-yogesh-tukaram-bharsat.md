Jhabua sits in the westernmost corner of Madhya Pradesh, bordering Gujarat and Rajasthan, with a population that is over 85 percent tribal — predominantly Bhil and Bhilala communities. By most social development indices it ranks among the state's most disadvantaged districts. It also carries a disproportionate burden of sickle cell disease, the inherited blood disorder that affects haemoglobin and causes chronic pain, organ damage and shortened lives. Sickle cell is not curable, but it is manageable — with consistent follow-up, access to welfare schemes and someone who knows your name and situation.

That last part is what the Sickle Mitra programme is trying to provide. Launched by District Collector Yogesh Tukaram Bharsat in early July 2026, the initiative assigns a dedicated volunteer — a Sickle Mitra — to each of the **1,950 registered sickle cell patients** in Jhabua. At the time of announcement, **290 Sickle Mitras** had already been enrolled. Their job is practical: assist with treatment compliance, connect patients to welfare schemes they qualify for, provide counselling, and ensure that no registered patient falls through the cracks between diagnoses.

## What the Programme Adds

Bharsat, a 2017-batch IAS officer who took charge of Jhabua in April 2026, is working within a broader state and national framework. Madhya Pradesh launched a Sickle Mitra initiative on **19 June 2026 — International Sickle Cell Day** — and President Droupadi Murmu praised the state's sickle cell screening drive at Omkareshwar around the same time, recognising the scale of the effort across tribal Madhya Pradesh.

But a statewide programme requires district-level execution, and that is where the collector's role becomes concrete. Jhabua's Sickle Mitra rollout means each patient in the district's registry now has a named individual responsible for their follow-up. For communities where healthcare access is already difficult, and where traditional healers have sometimes been the only option, that kind of structured accompaniment addresses a gap that diagnosis alone does not close.

Bharsat has also moved on other district infrastructure in the same period. He ordered an audit of **14,906 handpumps** across Jhabua, to be colour-coded red, yellow or green by functional status — a systematic attempt to map the district's actual water access rather than work from assumed figures. Separately, he directed officials to resolve **1,392 pending Level-1 CM Helpline complaints** within a week, with each case to be addressed on its merits rather than closed administratively.

The pattern across these initiatives is the same: take a number that represents a real need, put a specific person or process behind it, and track whether it gets done.

## Why It Matters for Madhya Pradesh

Sickle cell disease has a long history of underfunding and under-recognition in India, even as tribal populations carry some of the highest rates globally. A national sickle cell elimination mission is now formally underway, and Madhya Pradesh — with a large tribal population concentrated in districts like Jhabua, Alirajpur and Barwani — is central to it.

What Bharsat has done in Jhabua is convert a patient registry into a care network, one volunteer per patient. Whether the programme sustains and scales will depend on what happens over the next months. But for 1,950 people in one of Madhya Pradesh's most underserved districts, it has already changed what the registration means.
