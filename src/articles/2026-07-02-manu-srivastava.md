Madhya Pradesh did something remarkable last week that barely registered outside the specialist press. On 29 June 2026, Rewa Ultra Mega Solar Limited — the state's solar development company — commissioned **950 megawatts** of new solar capacity in a single announcement: a **500 MW park at Neemuch** and a **450 MW park at Shajapur**, both producing power at tariffs that set new national benchmarks. A day later, RUMSL signed a Power Purchase Agreement for **440 MW** of solar-plus-battery-storage at Morena at a historic **₹2.70 per unit** — the lowest tariff ever recorded for a project that guarantees round-the-clock renewable power delivery. Sixteen domestic and international developers had bid for the Morena contract.

Behind both milestones is Manu Srivastava, an IIT Delhi alumnus who joined the IAS in 1991 and has spent most of the last decade rebuilding Madhya Pradesh's energy architecture from a creaking dependence on coal into one of India's renewable energy leaders.

## A Decade of Methodical Work

Srivastava set the template with the Rewa Ultra Mega Solar Project, which when it came online delivered the lowest solar tariff India had ever seen and broke the grid-parity barrier — the first solar project in the country to supply institutional power at a rate below coal. Delhi Metro draws 65 per cent of its daytime energy from Rewa's 750 MW. The project earned the World Bank Group's President's Award and attracted study delegations from across South Asia.

What followed was replication at scale. RUMSL, which Srivastava chairs, developed the sprawling Agar-Shajapur-Neemuch solar complex, the 278 MW floating solar project on the Omkareshwar reservoir, and a growing series of hybrid renewable projects. Madhya Pradesh's total installed renewable capacity has crossed **12,018 MW**, now accounting for roughly 30 per cent of the state's electricity generation — a transformation from a state that barely featured in India's renewable energy tables a decade ago.

The most forward-looking move of the week came on 26 June 2026, when RUMSL floated India's first **technology-agnostic 24×7 renewable energy tender**. Unlike conventional tenders that prescribe solar or wind, this one invites developers to propose any combination of renewable sources — solar, wind, pumped storage, battery — so long as they guarantee firm round-the-clock power at a reliable tariff. A pre-bid meeting was held on 1 July.

The approach reflects a conviction that the next frontier for clean energy is not simply building more panels but solving intermittency: ensuring the grid runs on renewables after sundown and on still days. It is the kind of structural problem that gets solved in tender documents and bid conditions, not in speeches.

Madhya Pradesh sits at the heart of India's electricity map, and Srivastava's work at RUMSL has demonstrated that a state public sector company — operating without the venture capital glamour of private players — can drive an energy transition faster and at lower cost than most competitors managed. The fact that sixteen international and domestic developers competed for the Morena tender is perhaps the clearest measure of the institution he has built: the world's solar industry now watches what an IAS officer in Bhopal has been quietly building for ten years.
