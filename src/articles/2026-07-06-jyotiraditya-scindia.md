Jyotiraditya Scindia has spent two decades in national politics advocating for the Gwalior-Chambal region — and on 5 July 2026, he stood in Shivpuri to mark what may be the most consequential investment that belt has ever seen.

Alongside Madhya Pradesh Chief Minister Mohan Yadav and Adani Group managing director Jeet Adani, Scindia laid the foundation stone for a ₹**2,500-crore** missile manufacturing complex being built by Adani Defence and Aerospace. The plant is described as South Asia's largest private-sector missile ecosystem, and it is being built not in a major industrial corridor but in the Chambal plateau — a region more often associated with dacoity legends than defence contracts.

"Whenever it comes to the security of the country in the future, Shivpuri will be identified as a centre of defence production," Scindia said at the ceremony. "The weapons and ammunition produced here will enhance the strength of the Indian Army and showcase the contribution of the skills of the local youth in the defence of the nation."

## What the Shivpuri Plant Will Produce

The facility closes a gap that has long constrained India's domestic defence supply chain: no single private-sector site in the country has combined missile system assembly with in-house production of composite propellant and TNT. The Shivpuri complex will be the first to do all three at one location.

The missiles earmarked for production here include the Next Generation Anti-Radiation Missile (NGARM), RUDRAM-II, the Naval Anti-Ship Missile–Short Range (NASM-SR), the Long-Range Glide Bomb 'Gaurav', and the Tactical Autonomous Reconnaissance and Attack (TARA) system — a list that covers air, sea and ground combat requirements of the Indian armed forces.

The investment of **₹2,500 crore** is to be deployed over three years. It will generate **5,000** direct and indirect skilled jobs and draw more than **50** MSMEs into a specialised defence supply chain in the surrounding area. Scindia underscored the plant's continental significance: "This represents the largest investment by a private sector company not just in India, but in all of South Asia."

Shivpuri is not an isolated choice. Gwalior, 115 kilometres to the north, already houses a Small Arms Manufacturing Complex. Together the two facilities begin to sketch a coherent defence manufacturing corridor across the Chambal plateau — something Scindia has pushed for through his parliamentary tenures in Guna and Gwalior.

## Why It Matters for MP

Madhya Pradesh has spent the past few years making the case that it is more than a transit state — that it can anchor industrial and strategic investment at scale. The Shivpuri groundbreaking is one of the clearest proofs of that case yet.

For a Union Minister whose current portfolio is Communications and the development of India's northeastern frontier, championing a defence plant in his own Gwalior-Chambal constituency is a reminder that the most durable regional advocacy happens when a politician's personal roots run deep. Scindia's grandfather Jiwajirao Scindia was the last ruler of the Gwalior princely state; the family's connection to this plateau is multigenerational. The missile factory is a new chapter in a very old story.

When the Shivpuri complex reaches full production, weapons assembled there will enter the inventory of the Indian Army bearing no visible mark of the region that made them. Scindia and the Shivpuri workers who build them will know differently.
