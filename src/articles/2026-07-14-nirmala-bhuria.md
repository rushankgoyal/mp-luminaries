On the morning of 13 July 2026, Nirmala Bhuria stood in Petlawad — the Jhabua constituency she has held without interruption for five consecutive terms — and inaugurated development works worth **₹799.4 crore**. The occasion covered a wide range of projects: a road from Tejaji Temple to Phuta Temple along the Pampavati River, a Muktidham garden under the Amrit 2.0 scheme, a community hall in Ward No. 8, and an English-medium hostel for students in the area.

Among those projects was one with consequences stretching well beyond the municipal council boundary. Bhuria performed the bhoomi pujan for a new barrage at village Khardu Chhoti, estimated at **₹4.65 crore**, which will provide reliable irrigation to approximately **280 hectares** of agricultural land once complete. In a district like Jhabua — predominantly tribal, primarily dependent on seasonal rainfall, and among MP's most economically fragile — that kind of water infrastructure can shift a farming family's entire crop calendar.

Addressing the gathering, the minister said her efforts for the constituency's development were continuous and that she considered the area her own family. It was not an unusual sentiment for a politician who has won Petlawad five times; it was also not an abstract one, given the scale of projects the occasion represented.

## Five Terms, One Consistent Portfolio

Bhuria's story in Madhya Pradesh politics begins with her father, veteran tribal leader Dilip Singh Bhuria, and has followed a thread of firsts: she became the state's first tribal woman Cabinet Minister when the BJP government appointed her to the Women and Child Development portfolio. The WCD brief has proved consequential in her hands.

At a UNICEF-backed child budgeting workshop in Bhopal in May 2026, she laid out the government's numbers clearly: the 2026-27 state budget had directed **₹23,747 crore** to health and nutrition schemes — including Poshan 2.0 — a **26 per cent increase** in child-related allocations over the previous year, with **13.7 per cent** of total state expenditure earmarked for education. Her argument at that workshop was that child development cannot be managed within a single department; it requires coordination across health, education, water, and social welfare, and she pushed for integrated budgeting to reflect that reality.

In March 2026, she launched the AI Futures initiative, whose goal was to position rural women not as technology adopters but as technology builders — people creating AI tools, not merely using them. It was the kind of programme that sits oddly on a list of barrage inaugurations and Muktidham gardens; but that is the range of the WCD portfolio when pursued seriously.

What 13 July in Petlawad illustrated was the convergence of both tracks. The barrage at Khardu Chhoti will not trend nationally. But for the farming families on those 280 hectares, it is a more direct expression of governance than any policy document — the kind of concrete, unglamorous work that turns a constituency into something more than a seat on an electoral map. Bhuria has won that seat five times; the projects inaugurated this week offer one explanation for why.
