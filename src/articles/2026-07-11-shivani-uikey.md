On 4 July 2026, Prime Minister Narendra Modi walked the production floor of CG Semi's chip facility in Sanand, Gujarat, guided by the women who operate the machines there. Many had come from tribal belts in Madhya Pradesh, Jharkhand and Chhattisgarh, most of them graduates of government Industrial Training Institutes. Shivani Uikey from Balaghat, Madhya Pradesh, was among them.

Afterwards, Modi described the encounter: "They explained every process with great enthusiasm and confidence. These girls, who come from ordinary families, studied in ordinary schools and received their education from ITIs, are today doing extraordinary work."

Balaghat is a forested district in eastern Madhya Pradesh, where the Gond community has traditionally depended on agriculture and forest produce. Industrial Training Institutes in such districts teach trade skills — electrical work, fitting, welding — and have long been understood as a path to ordinary factory jobs, not to advanced manufacturing. Shivani Uikey took that path, completed her ITI qualification, and found herself selected for something considerably beyond what those tracks usually lead to.

## From Balaghat to the Cleanroom

CG Semi Private Limited's Outsourced Semiconductor Assembly and Test (OSAT) facility at Sanand is India's **third semiconductor manufacturing site** to begin production under the India Semiconductor Mission, built with an investment of approximately **₹7,500 crore**. The plant assembles and tests chips for use across electronics sectors, with trained workers operating precision equipment under cleanroom conditions.

Before starting production, workers underwent specialised training in Malaysia, where semiconductor manufacturing has a decades-long tradition. Instruction was given partly in Hindi, making the technical content accessible to workers for whom English is not the primary language. The women returned ready to run the machines.

Shivani and her colleagues were on the floor when Modi inaugurated the plant on 4 July, marking the start of commercial production. The Prime Minister's visit turned an occasion that might otherwise have been a policy event into something more specific: a recognition that the workers themselves, from places like Balaghat, are what India's semiconductor push depends on.

> "Seeing our success, tears of happiness came to our parents' eyes. Together with CG Semi, we are going to create history."

Her parents' reaction is easy to understand. In a district where formal employment beyond agriculture is limited, placing a daughter in high-technology chip manufacturing — after state-funded ITI training and an international placement — represents a real change in what is considered possible.

## Why It Matters for Madhya Pradesh

India's semiconductor story is usually told through investment commitments, fab announcements and ministerial targets. The human pipeline that makes any of it functional tends to stay offscreen. Shivani Uikey's presence on that production floor is evidence that the pipeline works: ITI graduates from rural and tribal Madhya Pradesh, trained at government institutions and then sent abroad for exposure, can and do run the equipment when the ribbon is cut.

Madhya Pradesh's tribal districts — Balaghat, Mandla, Dindori, Seoni — have for years contributed workers to industries elsewhere in India. The semiconductor sector is a newer destination on that map, and it brings with it skills and wages significantly above what most departing workers previously accessed.

That shift is visible in the one detail Shivani chose to mention: her parents watching her succeed, and crying.
