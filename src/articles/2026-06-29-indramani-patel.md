In 2004, a truck loaded with bricks lost control on a ghat road outside Indore and plunged into the valley at Bharughat, roughly 25 kilometres from the city. The vehicle did not fall all the way. It caught on a tree, suspended over a gorge dropping **200 feet** to the rocks below. The driver and his helper were trapped inside. A professional crane operator assessed the situation and refused to descend into the gorge.

Sub-Inspector Indramani Patel, the Station House Officer at Simrol police station in Indore district, tied himself to a rope. He lowered himself into the gorge, reached the truck, secured the vehicle to the surrounding trees with ropes, and pulled both men to safety.

When his department submitted a case for out-of-turn promotion under Regulation 70-A of the Madhya Pradesh Police Regulations — the provision that exists precisely for such acts of exceptional bravery — the government rejected the claim in 2012. The cash reward Patel received for what may have prevented two deaths: **₹5,000**.

He filed a writ petition (WP 9716/2017) in the Madhya Pradesh High Court. In June 2026, Justice Vivek Kumar Singh delivered judgment in *Indramani Patel v. State of Madhya Pradesh* (2026 LiveLaw MP 210), quashing the 2012 rejection order and directing that Patel receive notional promotion to the rank of Inspector with retrospective effect from **10 February 2005**. The state was given 60 days to complete the exercise, with full consequential benefits — corrected seniority, reconsideration for further promotions — applied accordingly.

## What the Court Decided

Justice Singh's ruling rests on a precise distinction. The state had argued, in effect, that responding to emergencies is part of a police officer's duties, and that Patel's rescue therefore fell within his ordinary obligations. The court rejected this directly. A Sub-Inspector who enters a situation that a trained crane operator declined to enter — suspended over a 200-foot void on a rope, with a precariously balanced truck below — is not performing a routine function. The level of physical risk, the absence of any professional equipment, and the fact that no one else would go down: together these place the act in a separate category from standard police work. That is what Regulation 70-A exists to recognise.

The judgment also drew attention to a revealing contrast: the system that gave Patel a ₹5,000 cash award for potentially preventing a mass fatality had granted out-of-turn promotions in the same period for achievements in typing competitions. The court did not let that comparison pass quietly. It stands as a comment on how bureaucratic reward structures can become detached from the things they exist to honour.

For Patel, the ruling closes a 21-year gap between the act and its recognition. He made no public appeal; the process moved through internal channels and then, slowly, through the courts. The Madhya Pradesh High Court's June 2026 order does not erase two decades of withheld rank and seniority — those are consequential losses in any career — but it names clearly what he did: extraordinary bravery, not routine duty. That distinction, finally on record, is overdue.
