On 7 July 2026, Vishvas Sarang arrived at TT Nagar Stadium in Bhopal to meet 22 athletes who had qualified for the Asian Games in Aichi-Nagoya, Japan — one of the largest single-state contingents Madhya Pradesh has sent to a major multi-sport games. They came from nine disciplines: athletics, shooting, rifle shooting, canoe slalom, kayaking, canoeing, shotgun shooting, judo, and equestrian events.

"The selection of 22 athletes is a proud moment for the state," Sarang said at the meeting. "Their success is the result of hard work, dedicated coaches, and continuous support for sports in Madhya Pradesh."

As Minister for Co-operatives, Sports and Youth Welfare since 2023, Sarang, an MLA from Narela in Bhopal, has pressed the state's sports department to move on three parallel fronts: find young talent early, give them structured competition at every level, and invest in the academies that convert potential into performance. The July meeting was not a symbolic occasion — it was the number on the scoreboard.

## Three Programmes That Built the Pipeline

The most visible of Sarang's initiatives has been the Khelo MP Youth Games 2026, which ran from January onwards at block, district, divisional and state levels across **28 sports disciplines**. By the time the state-level finals closed at the Kushabhau Thakre Auditorium in Bhopal — with Governor Mangubhai Patel in attendance as chief guest — the games had provided thousands of young athletes across MP, from rural blocks to city districts, their first experience of organised multi-level competition.

At the same time, Sarang backed a National Rifle Association of India (NRAI) talent hunt programme aimed at introducing **7.5 lakh school and college students** to shooting. Speaking at the Kumar Surendra Singh Memorial competition at the MP Shooting Academy in Bhopal, he set a specific long-horizon goal: Madhya Pradesh will produce the maximum number of shooting medalists for India at the 2036 Olympics, which India is bidding to host. He has also stated his intent to bring the Shooting League of India — a world-class franchise competition — to Madhya Pradesh.

A third strand, Summer Camp Aroh-2026, provided athletes with structured pre-season training across disciplines in a single setting, compressing what athletes in other states often have to piece together from multiple sources.

## A Landlocked State That Competes on Water

The results from 2025 showed that the infrastructure investment was reaching even the most unlikely corners of the state's sports programme. In August 2025, at the inaugural Khelo India Water Sports Festival at Dal Lake in Srinagar, Madhya Pradesh's paddle-sports academy dominated the competition: the state won **10 of the 24 gold medals** on offer to claim the overall team championship, beating coastal and riverine states that might have been expected to hold an advantage. Athletes from the MP State Water Sports Academy in Bhopal won individual gold medals in both women's kayaking slalom and women's canoeing slalom.

For a state that has no coast and no natural whitewater of the kind found in the Himalayas, that result required decades of deliberate investment — in coaches, equipment, a training venue at the Lower Lake, and a competition calendar that keeps athletes match-ready year round.

The 22 Asian Games qualifiers span sports that rarely share a headline: a judoka, an equestrian rider, a rifle shooter, and a canoe paddler will all carry the state's name to Japan in September. That breadth — rather than depth in a single discipline — is what makes the 2026 contingent a measure of the programme's reach.
