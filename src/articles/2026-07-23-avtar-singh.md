Twelve years ago, a young judoka from Gurdaspur, Punjab, stepped onto the mat at the 2014 Commonwealth Games in Glasgow and lost in the round of sixteen to Scotland's Matthew Purssey. Today, **Avtar Singh** is back in the same city — older, stronger, and competing in the men's -100 kg event at the 2026 Commonwealth Games that opened at Glasgow's SEC Centre this morning.

Born on **3 April 1992**, Singh grew up in Gurdaspur and came to judo through the local wrestling and combat sports culture of northern Punjab. He stands 1.93 metres tall — an advantage in the gripping exchanges that define judo at the heavier weights — and has spent years refining that natural physical frame into precision technique at the SAI National Centre of Excellence in Bhopal, where he has trained under the national coaching setup.

## From Rio to Glasgow, Again

The chapter that defined Singh's career came in 2016. He qualified for the **Rio Olympics in the men's -90 kg category** — the first Indian judoka to reach the Olympic mat in twelve years, since Athens 2004. That qualification, earned through the continental ranking circuit, ended more than a decade of absence for Indian judo at the Games. He did not medal at Rio, but the feat of qualification alone reset what Indian judo believed was possible for its senior men.

He has competed steadily through the years since, navigating the weight-category transitions that come with ageing into the heavier divisions. In **2023**, competing on the IJF Grand Slam circuit, he took a bronze at the Asian Open in Kuwait City. In **2025**, he claimed silver at the Asian Open in Hong Kong — a result that put him in contention for the 2026 Commonwealth Games squad. When the four SAI NCOE Bhopal judokas were named for Glasgow, Singh was among them, selected for the men's -100 kg event.

The Glasgow return carries its own quiet symmetry. In 2014 he competed there as a junior on the rise; in 2026 he arrives as the most experienced Indian judoka at the Games, a veteran who has already stood at an Olympic podium area and knows what international pressure feels like at the highest level.

What Singh's presence illustrates about the SAI NCOE Bhopal is the same thing Asmita Dey's selection does: Madhya Pradesh's judo infrastructure has quietly become one of the more functional pipelines in Indian combat sports. Four judokas from one training centre, fielded across weight categories and genders, at a single Commonwealth Games — that is a programme result, not a coincidence. Singh is the senior figure in that group, the one whose career arc shows both what the pipeline produces and how long it can sustain a competitive athlete at the international level.
