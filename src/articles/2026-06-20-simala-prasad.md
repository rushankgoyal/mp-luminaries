When "The Narmada Story" released on June 12, 2026, it brought something unusual to cinema: the film's lead police officer is played by a serving Deputy Inspector General of Police. Simala Prasad is DIG of Police in Madhya Pradesh — and she chose to spend part of her leave on a film set in Narmadapuram, playing a sub-inspector navigating a case about a tribal mother fighting the system after her daughter is harmed.

The choice to play a junior officer was deliberate. Despite holding a senior rank, Prasad wanted to portray the specific pressures and moral weight carried by grassroots police officers — the people whose daily decisions determine whether ordinary Indians, especially women and adivasi communities, ever see justice. "Cinema spreads a message to millions," she has said. "You must use every platform available to you."

## From a Bhopal Classroom to the UPSC Rank List

Prasad grew up in Bhopal. Her father, Dr. Bhagirath Prasad, is a retired IAS officer and former Lok Sabha member; her mother, Mehrunnisa Parvez, is an accomplished writer. That household — part civil service, part literature — shaped the path she would take. She studied at St. Joseph Co-ed School in Bhopal, earned a Bachelor's in Commerce and a Master's in Sociology from Barkatullah University, and then cleared the UPSC Civil Services Examination on her very first attempt, without coaching, with an All India Rank of 51. She joined the Indian Police Service and was allotted the Madhya Pradesh cadre.

Before the civil services, she had already acted in Bollywood films — and the director of those earlier films, Zaigham Imam, is the same person who made "The Narmada Story." It is a long creative collaboration rooted in shared respect: Imam has said he cast Prasad not despite her identity as a police officer but because of it. A real IPS officer playing an investigating officer in a film about policing gives the story a ground it could not get from a professional actor alone.

## The Story the Film Is Telling

"The Narmada Story" is set in and around Narmadapuram in Madhya Pradesh — an area where the Narmada river anchors the lives of tribal communities and where the gap between what the law promises and what it delivers can be very wide. The film follows a tribal mother named Agni Dhurve who decides to fight the system after something terrible happens to her daughter. Prasad's character, sub-inspector Narmada Raikwar, takes on the investigation.

The film was shot entirely in Narmadapuram and surrounding regions, using local landscapes and real settings rather than studio recreations. Director Zaigham Imam met Chief Minister Dr. Mohan Yadav during promotions — the film has been positioned as a story that celebrates Madhya Pradesh's people, its women officers, and its cultural landscape.

For Prasad, the role is a continuation of her actual work. As a senior IPS officer focused on women's safety, she investigates exactly the kinds of cases that "The Narmada Story" dramatises. The film does not stray from her professional identity — it extends it into a medium that reaches audiences her uniform never could.
