Yuvraj Singh Rathore travelled from Chhota Undwa village in Alirajpur district — deep in Madhya Pradesh's southern tribal belt — to the Indira Gandhi Pratishthan in Lucknow last week. He came back with seven first prizes from the UP Mango Festival 2026, competing against growers from every corner of the country.

Rathore entered eight mango varieties and won first prize in seven of them: Fazli, Romani, Noorjahan, Rajapuri, Kesar, Manohari and Hapus. His Chausa entry earned second place. Uttar Pradesh Horticulture Minister Dinesh Pratap Singh presented the awards at the three-day festival, which distributed **147 trophies** in total. The Alirajpur District Administration and Agriculture Department publicly congratulated Rathore, saying his achievement would inspire more farmers in the district to adopt scientific and modern farming practices.

## A 140-Variety Orchard in One of India's Most Tribal Districts

Alirajpur, tucked into the corner where Madhya Pradesh meets Gujarat and Rajasthan, is one of India's most tribal-majority districts. Growing paddy, wheat or maize on small plots is the norm. Rathore has spent decades doing something very different on his Chhota Undwa farm: collecting, growing and conserving around **140 mango varieties** — some nationally renowned, some rare regional cultivars that might otherwise disappear.

That collection did not happen by chance. Rathore worked closely with scientists at Rajmata Vijayaraje Scindia Agricultural University and the Indian Council of Agricultural Research (ICAR) to master grafting, pest management and post-harvest handling. The combination of conservationist instinct and research-backed agronomy explains why eight of his entries entered Lucknow and seven came home with gold.

His farm is also a serious enterprise. Rathore sells **₹20–22 lakh** worth of mangoes annually to buyers in Madhya Pradesh, Delhi, Maharashtra and Himachal Pradesh. Beyond his own fields, he mentors neighbouring farmers and regularly encourages young people in Alirajpur to try scientific horticulture — pointing to his own orchard as proof that high-value farming is possible even in the most remote corners of the state.

For a district where agricultural income data consistently runs below the state average, Rathore represents something rare: durable, self-built prosperity rooted in both indigenous knowledge and modern science. His seven trophies from Lucknow are not just personal prizes — they announce that Alirajpur grows world-class mangoes, and that one man's patient decades of work can put a forgotten corner of Madhya Pradesh on the national horticultural map.
