Every Indian startup myth begins in Bangalore or Gurugram. Nemesh Singh's begins in a bedroom in Bhopal, at a computer his father gifted him when he was a mechanical engineering student who couldn't land a job after graduation.

That computer became **Appointy**, the online appointment-scheduling platform Singh launched in 2007 — and which today serves **more than 100,000 businesses across over 100 countries**, from salons in São Paulo to clinics in Singapore. Most remarkably, it got there **without venture capital and largely without a sales team**: the product, priced from $10 a month after Singh was forced to start charging for it, simply sold itself.

## Bankrupt twice, beaten never

The "forced" part is the heart of the story. Appointy went **bankrupt twice** in its early years. A graduate of Bhopal's Oriental Institute of Science and Technology with no family money behind the business, Singh responded not by folding but by repricing, rebuilding, and grinding forward. Recognition eventually followed the resilience: **NASSCOM named Appointy one of India's top 50 emerging companies in 2013**, and Microsoft selected it for its accelerator programme's summer 2014 batch.

Through all of it, Singh refused the standard advice to relocate to a metro. Appointy stayed in Bhopal, hired in Bhopal, and is now putting down even deeper roots — a **four-acre campus** in the city — while Singh spends part of his time mentoring the next generation of Bhopal founders so they can skip the mistakes he paid for in full.

Madhya Pradesh wants 10,000 registered startups in the coming years. Policy funds and incubators will help. But what a young founder in Gwalior or Rewa needs most is proof that it can be done from here — and Nemesh Singh's two-decade experiment is exactly that proof: world-class software, built at home, owned outright.
