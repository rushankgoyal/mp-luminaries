When Justice Sanjeev Sachdeva was sworn in as a judge of the **Supreme Court of India on June 2, 2026**, he had spent more than a decade on the benches of two High Courts — the last eleven months of which were the most senior posting in his career: Chief Justice of the Madhya Pradesh High Court in Jabalpur. He will serve on the nation's highest bench until his retirement on December 25, 2029, a tenure in which some of the most consequential questions in Indian constitutional and civil law will come before him.

Sachdeva grew up in Delhi. He completed his schooling at Delhi Public School, Mathura Road, in 1982, then studied commerce at Sri Ram College of Commerce (SRCC), University of Delhi, graduating in 1985. His law degree came from Campus Law Centre in 1988, where he placed on the merit list. In 1992, he was one of five young Indian lawyers chosen for the Commonwealth Young Lawyers Course at the Institute of Advanced Legal Studies at the University of London, funded by a British Council Scholarship — an early recognition that placed him in a cohort of practitioners headed for senior positions in the Indian legal system. He qualified as an Advocate-on-Record in the Supreme Court in 1995, and the Delhi High Court designated him a Senior Advocate in 2011, a distinction the court extends to a very small fraction of practising lawyers.

## Eleven Months in Jabalpur

The Supreme Court collegium transferred Sachdeva from the Delhi High Court — where he had served as a judge since April 2013 — to the Madhya Pradesh High Court and elevated him as its **29th Chief Justice on July 17, 2025**. In Jabalpur, he presided over a court that covers Madhya Pradesh and hears matters affecting tens of millions of people across central India.

One order that drew national legal attention came in the case of *Ashutosh Dixit v State of Madhya Pradesh*. A Division Bench led by Chief Justice Sachdeva and Justice Vinay Saraf initiated suo motu criminal contempt proceedings against sitting BJP MLA Sanjay Pathak from Vijayraghavgarh, after Justice Vishal Mishra placed on record that Pathak had telephoned him regarding an illegal mining case — involving the alleged recovery of more than **₹443 crore** from companies linked to his family — that was pending before him. The bench called it a prima facie act of criminal contempt and ordered proceedings to begin. LiveLaw described it as likely among the first cases of its kind, where a High Court ordered contempt proceedings after a sitting judge recorded a sitting legislator's attempt to contact him about a live matter. The case drew coverage across the legal press and was cited as an example of judicial independence in action.

The Supreme Court collegium recommended Sachdeva's elevation on **May 28, 2026**, alongside four other Chief Justices and a Senior Advocate. He was sworn in five days later.

For the Madhya Pradesh High Court, the elevation of its most recent Chief Justice to the Supreme Court confirms the standing that the Jabalpur bench has built over decades. The court has produced significant judgments on forest rights, land law, and constitutional questions with direct relevance to rural and tribal India. Sachdeva spent eleven months shaping that institution's direction — and his most visible act there, the contempt order, said clearly that access to judges is not a matter for political negotiation.

He arrives at the Supreme Court with a practitioner's depth in commercial and constitutional law, a decade of High Court experience, and a stint as Chief Justice that, in fewer than a year, left a mark on the record of judicial independence in India's heartland.
