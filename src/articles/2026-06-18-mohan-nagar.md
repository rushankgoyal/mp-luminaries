When President Droupadi Murmu presented Mohan Nagar the Padma Shri at Rashtrapati Bhavan on **May 25, 2026**, the citation read "social work" — two words for thirty years of work in Betul's drylands: village meetings, demonstration ponds, eroded hillsides replanted, and water tables that began to rise again where they had been falling for decades. Nagar, born on February 23, 1968 in Betul district, had already been called the Water Man of Betul by the communities whose harvests and daily lives his work had changed. India's fourth-highest civilian honour said the same thing in different language.

Betul district sits on the Satpura plateau in southern Madhya Pradesh, where summer rains arrive in concentrated bursts and run off quickly through bare, eroded soil. Villages that receive adequate monsoon rainfall can still run dry by February. Groundwater tables had been dropping across the district for a generation before Nagar turned to this problem full-time. He completed a master's degree in Political Science at Vikram University in Ujjain, returned to Betul, and stayed. For more than three decades he has worked through the Bharat Bharti Education Institute in Betul and serves as Vice-President of the **Madhya Pradesh Jan Abhiyan Parishad**, the state-government-linked body that coordinates grassroots development programmes across MP.

## The Ganga Avataran Campaign

The programme Nagar is most closely associated with is the Ganga Avataran Campaign — a community-led initiative that uses rainwater harvesting and groundwater recharge to restore local water sources. The name invokes the Hindu tradition of the Ganga's descent to earth, and Nagar used that imagery deliberately: to persuade tribal and rural communities in Betul's Vanvasi belt that protecting their local rivers and ponds was both practical and spiritually meaningful.

Working with village-level groups over three decades, his teams built check dams on seasonal streams, deepened traditional village ponds, constructed percolation pits across degraded slopes, and replanted tree cover on hillsides so that monsoon water would be absorbed rather than lost to runoff. Wells that had dried out returned to water. Fields that had produced only a single monsoon crop began supporting irrigated winter cultivation. The effort revived water sources across a stretch of southern Madhya Pradesh that has long been considered structurally drought-prone.

The work also shaped how the state thinks about water governance. When the Mohan Yadav government launched the Jal Ganga Sanvardhan Abhiyan — an MP-wide water conservation mission that pushed the state to third in national rankings in 2025 — it drew on a participatory model that grassroots organisers like Nagar had been refining since the 1990s. His approach was consistent: not infrastructure delivered from outside, but communities trained to understand their own watershed and take responsibility for it.

Thousands of farming families across Betul's tribal belt now have year-round access to water that Nagar's campaign helped restore. Healthier water tables mean year-round irrigated farming instead of a single crop season. The Padma Shri places him among India's formally recognised contributors to environmental welfare. For Betul's villages, the recognition confirms what their full wells and standing crops already demonstrated.
