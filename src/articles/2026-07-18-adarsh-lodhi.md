When the National Testing Agency declared NEET-UG 2026 results on 17 July, Adarsh Lodhi refreshed the results page and saw what the past two years had been working toward: **680 out of 720**, an All India Rank of 304, and a category rank of 79. He is eighteen years old. His parents are daily-wage labourers who work in other people's fields in Raisen district, one of Madhya Pradesh's more rural heartlands.

There was no coaching institute. There was no private tutor and no supplementary app subscription. There was a scholarship seat at the Government Subhash Higher Secondary School for Excellence in Bhopal — one of the state's few public institutions that regularly sends students to medical colleges — and there was a discipline Lodhi had set for himself before he ever arrived at that school: thirteen hours of study every day, and no smartphone.

Not reduced smartphone use — none. Adarsh Lodhi chose a basic handset for essential calls only, reasoning that the notifications and short-form content that fill a smartphone's screen would eat exactly the hours that he needed for Biology, Chemistry and Physics. In the two years since he made that choice, the reasoning held.

## A Scholarship That Changed the Arithmetic

The pathway from a Raisen village to a government school in Bhopal is not automatic. It requires clearing a competitive selection process, and it requires a family willing to support a child who will live away from home while they study. Adarsh Lodhi's parents — both agricultural labourers — made that call.

Government Subhash Higher Secondary School for Excellence operates on the premise that the state's best public-school students, if given the right environment, can compete for the country's highest-stakes entrance examinations. In 2026, the school produced **23 NEET qualifiers**. Lodhi was among them — and ranked among the highest of that cohort nationally.

The NEET-UG 2026 re-examination (following the June 21 paper) had a competitive field running into hundreds of thousands of candidates. A score of 680/720 places Lodhi in a range where AIIMS Delhi — his first choice — is a realistic target. AIIMS Bhopal is his second.

## What the Score Means

A rank of 304 nationally from a scholarship seat at a government school in a state where private medical coaching routinely costs several lakhs per year is not a coincidence. It is the product of a very specific set of choices: where to study, how many hours, which distractions to eliminate.

For Madhya Pradesh, Adarsh Lodhi's result is a useful reminder of what the Excellence School system — which the state has invested in to provide quality secondary education outside the private sector — is capable of producing when students have the determination to use it. His family's circumstances did not give him a head start. He arrived at the finish line by changing the hours-per-day arithmetic and holding that calculation for two years straight.

He wants to be a doctor. With a rank of 304 and AIIMS Delhi in range, Raisen district may soon have a reason to mark that on the map.
